## Question

How can ambient PM2.5 exposure amplify inflammatory signaling in genetically susceptible asthma populations through an IL33-linked NF-kB mechanism?

## Evidence summary

- Asthma GWAS meta-analyses repeatedly identify IL33-region loci as high-confidence association signals.
- Exposure literature links PM2.5 burden with worsened asthma outcomes at the population level.
- GTEx-derived eQTL context supports tissue-relevant IL33 expression in lung-associated compartments.
- Pathway evidence places NF-kB as a central inflammatory regulator responsive to oxidative stress.

## Mechanistic chain

1. PM2.5 inhalation increases oxidative stress at the bronchial epithelial interface.
2. ROS-associated signaling activates IKK complexes and downstream NF-kB transcriptional programs.
3. NF-kB activation amplifies epithelial danger signaling and IL33 release.
4. IL33-ST2 signaling engages ILC2 and mast-cell pathways, increasing type-2 inflammatory cytokines.
5. Heightened cytokine signaling contributes to eosinophilic inflammation and airway hyperresponsiveness in susceptible populations.

## Tissue specificity

Primary evidence centers on bronchial epithelium and airway immune niches, with secondary inflammatory amplification in regional lymphoid tissue. Existing human tissue data is improving but still limited relative to in vitro and animal systems.

## Counterarguments and limitations

- Several mechanistic steps rely on convergent but non-identical study designs, reducing certainty in direct causal ordering.
- A substantial portion of GWAS evidence remains European-dominated, limiting transferability confidence across all ancestry groups.
- Exposure misclassification can occur when regional monitor data does not fully represent personal microenvironment variability.

## What would validate this

- Prospective bronchial biopsy studies pairing measured PM2.5 burden with IL33 expression and NF-kB readouts.
- Replicated exposure-aware eQTL analyses in multi-ancestry cohorts.
- Controlled perturbation experiments in primary human airway epithelial systems with pre-registered outcome criteria.
